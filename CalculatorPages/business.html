The Fundamentals of Small Business Finance

Runway & Burn Rate: The Pulse of your Startup

For any small business or startup, cash flow is the ultimate metric of survival. Burn Rate refers to the amount of money your company is losing each month, while Runway is the number of months you can continue operating before running out of cash. Our calculator helps you visualize this critical timeline. A high burn rate isn't necessarily bad if it's fueling high growth, but it must be managed with absolute precision. Many businesses fail not because they lacked a good product, but because they simply ran out of time to find their market fit.

To extend your runway, you have two levers: increase revenue or decrease expenses. In the early stages, focus on "Net Burn" (Total Expenses minus Total Revenue). Monitoring this figure monthly allows you to make data-driven decisions about hiring, marketing spend, and when to start your next fundraising round. A safe runway is typically considered 12-18 months, giving you enough time to pivot if your initial strategy hits a roadblock.

The Break-Even Point: From Hobby to Business

The Break-Even Point is the magical moment when your total revenue equals your total expenses. Before this point, you are losing money; after it, you are generating profit. Our tool calculates the exact number of units you need to sell to cover your fixed costs (rent, salaries, software) based on your gross margin per unit. This is a fundamental "sanity check" for any business plan. If you need to sell 10,000 units a month to break even, but the total market is only 5,000 people, your business model needs a fundamental redesign.

Understanding your break-even point also allows you to perform "sensitivity analysis." What happens if your supplier raises prices by 10%? What if you lower your price to attract more customers? By modeling these scenarios, you can find the most resilient path to profitability and set realistic sales targets for your team.

LTV / CAC: The Unit Economics of Growth

If you want to scale a business, you must master the ratio between Customer Lifetime Value (LTV) and Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC). CAC is what you spend on marketing and sales to get a new customer, while LTV is the total profit you expect to earn from that customer over their entire relationship with you. A healthy business typically aims for an LTV/CAC ratio of 3:1 or higher. If your ratio is 1:1, you are essentially "buying" revenue at no profit, which is unsustainable in the long term.

Our calculator helps you find this ratio by modeling your churn rate and average order value. If your CAC is too high, focus on organic growth channels or improving your sales funnel conversion rate. If your LTV is too low, focus on customer retention, upselling, or increasing your prices. This ratio is the first thing any sophisticated investor will look at when evaluating your business's scalability.

Margin vs. Markup: Don't Confuse Them

One of the most common mistakes in small business is confusing Profit Margin with Markup. Markup is the percentage added to the cost to reach a selling price, while Margin is the percentage of the selling price that is profit. If you buy something for $100 and sell it for $150, your markup is 50%, but your margin is only 33.3%. Confusing these two can lead to disastrous pricing errors that eat away at your bottom line.
