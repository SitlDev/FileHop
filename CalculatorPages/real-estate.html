The Fundamentals of Real Estate Investment

Rent vs. Buy: The 7-Year Rule

Deciding whether to rent or buy a home is one of the most significant financial decisions you'll ever make. While buying is often seen as "building equity," it also comes with substantial upfront costs like down payments, closing costs, and moving expenses. Our Rent vs. Buy Calculator helps you find the "break-even point"—the number of years you must live in a home for the cost of ownership to be lower than the cost of renting. In many markets, this is the "7-Year Rule." If you plan to move in less than five years, renting is often the smarter financial move due to the high transaction costs of selling a home (typically 6% in agent commissions).

Don't forget the "hidden costs" of ownership. When you rent, your monthly payment is the maximum you'll pay for housing. When you own, your mortgage payment is the minimum. You are responsible for property taxes, homeowners insurance, and maintenance—which is generally estimated at 1% of the home's value per year. Buying a home is as much a lifestyle choice as it is a financial one; ensure the numbers align with your long-term goals before signing on the dotted line.

Affordability: The 28/36 Rule

Banks use specific ratios to determine how much they are willing to lend you. The most common is the 28/36 Rule: your mortgage payment should not exceed 28% of your gross monthly income, and your total debt payments (including car loans and credit cards) should not exceed 36%. Our Home Affordability Tool provides two estimates: a "Conservative" budget that sticks to these rules, and an "Aggressive" budget that pushes the limits. Being "house poor" can limit your ability to save for retirement or handle unexpected emergencies, so we recommend staying closer to the conservative side whenever possible.

Additionally, consider the impact of your interest rate. Even a 1% difference in your mortgage rate can cost you tens of thousands of dollars over the life of a 30-year loan. Improving your credit score before you apply for a mortgage is one of the highest-leverage activities you can perform to increase your purchasing power and lower your long-term costs.

Rental ROI: Understanding the Cap Rate

For investors, the most important metric is the Cap Rate (Capitalization Rate). This is the ratio of the property's Net Operating Income (NOI) to its purchase price. It allows you to compare different properties regardless of how they are financed. A "good" cap rate varies by market—in a high-growth city like Austin, a 4% cap rate might be acceptable, while in a more stable market, investors might look for 7-8%. Our Rental ROI Calculator automates this calculation, helping you quickly filter through potential investment properties to find those that meet your cash flow requirements.

Closing Costs: The Final Hurdle

Many first-time buyers are surprised by the amount of cash needed at the closing table beyond their down payment. Closing Costs typically range from 2% to 5% of the purchase price and include things like loan origination fees, title insurance, appraisal fees, and government recording taxes. Our estimator helps you budget for these "frictional costs" so you don't have any unpleasant surprises in the final days of your home purchase. Always ask for a "Loan Estimate" (LE) from your lender early in the process to get a detailed breakdown of these fees.
