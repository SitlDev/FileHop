World Tea Cultures

Tea is humanity's most universal ritual — yet each culture has made it utterly its own. Eight traditions, one leaf, infinite expression.

🇯🇵
Japanese Chadō
The Way of Tea

A spiritual discipline through tea preparation — rooted in Zen Buddhism, expressing harmony, respect, purity, and tranquility.

🇨🇳
Chinese Gongfu Cha
Skill & Devotion

The art of brewing tea with great skill — using small clay pots, multiple rapid steeps, and meditative focus to extract every nuance.

🇲🇦
Moroccan Atay
Hospitality as Ceremony

Gunpowder green tea, spearmint, sugar — poured from height for froth. Three glasses: life, love, death. Refusal is impolite.

🇮🇳
Indian Chai Wallah
Tea of the Streets

Spiced, milky, sweetened CTC black tea simmered roadside — the heartbeat of Indian daily life from Mumbai to Kolkata.

🇬🇧
British High Tea
Civilized Ritual

The Victorian institution of afternoon tea — tiered stands, finger sandwiches, scones with clotted cream, and the eternal milk-first debate.

🇹🇷
Turkish Çay
The Social Glue

Rize black tea brewed in a double-stacked çaydanlık — drunk from tulip glasses, offered everywhere from bazaars to barbershops.

🇹🇼
Taiwanese Tea Culture
From Tradition to Innovation

Home of high-mountain oolongs and bubble tea — Taiwan seamlessly blends reverence for ancient gongfu cha with playful modern invention.

🇰🇪
Kenyan Chai
A New World Tradition

Boiled black tea with milk and sugar — Kenya's own chai culture, borne of the colonial tea industry yet now wholly, joyfully Kenyan.

Brewing Science

Every variable in brewing is a dial — adjust temperature, time, ratio, or water quality and the same leaf becomes a different experience. This is the science of extraction.

🎛️ Interactive Brew Calculator

80°C
Green: 70-80°C · Oolong: 85-95°C · Black: 95-100°C
3g / 150ml
Western: 2-3g · Gongfu: 6-10g
90s
Gongfu: 15-45s · Western: 2-5 min
1
Rinse the Vessel

Pour boiling water into your teapot or gaiwan. Swirl and discard. This preheats the vessel and eliminates any unwanted aromas.

2
Rinse the Leaf

For compressed or aged teas only: add leaves, cover with hot water for 5–10 seconds, discard. This opens the leaf and removes surface dust.

3
First Steep

Pour water in a circular motion to agitate the leaves gently. Cover and wait your calculated steep time. Pour completely — residual water over-extracts.

4
Subsequent Steeps

Add 10–15 seconds per additional steep for Western method. Fine teas reward 5–10 re-steeps, each revealing a new facet of the leaf.

5
Appreciate the Liquor

Observe the colour. Smell before sipping. Feel the texture. Let the finish linger. Tea rewards those who slow down.
